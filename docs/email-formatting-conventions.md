# Email & Message Formatting Conventions — Research

Research compiled from Purdue OWL, WikiHow (etiquette coach-reviewed), and plain language guidelines. Purpose: define deterministic rules for Dikta's post-transcription formatter.

---

## 1. Email/Message Structure (6 Distinct Zones)

Every email/message has up to 6 structural zones, each separated by a blank line:

```
Zone 1: Greeting          (always its own line)
Zone 2: Opening pleasantry (optional, its own line or paragraph)
Zone 3: Body              (one or more paragraphs)
Zone 4: Closing pleasantry (optional, its own line)
Zone 5: Sign-off          (its own line, followed by comma)
Zone 6: Name/Signature    (its own line, directly below sign-off)
```

### Zone boundaries — blank lines

- **Blank line** after greeting (before body or opening pleasantry)
- **Blank line** between each body paragraph
- **Blank line** before closing pleasantry (separates it from body)
- **NO blank line** between sign-off and name (they are a unit)

---

## 2. Zone 1 — Greeting / Salutation

### Rules
- ALWAYS on its own line, even bare "Hi" or "Hello" without a name
- Followed by a comma (informal) or colon (formal)
- Blank line after greeting before next zone

### Patterns (detect these at start of message)
| Pattern | Formality | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| `Hi,` | Casual | Hi, |
| `Hi [Name],` | Casual | Hi Anna, |
| `Hey,` / `Hey [Name],` | Very casual | Hey Marcus, |
| `Hello,` / `Hello [Name],` | Neutral | Hello, |
| `Dear [Name],` | Formal | Dear Mr. Johnson, |
| `Good morning/afternoon/evening,` | Semi-formal | Good morning, |
| `Good morning [Name],` | Semi-formal | Good morning Sarah, |
| `To whom it may concern,` | Very formal | To whom it may concern, |

### Detection keywords
```
hi, hey, hello, dear, good morning, good afternoon, good evening,
to whom it may concern, greetings
```

---

## 3. Zone 2 — Opening Pleasantry (Optional)

### Rules
- Goes on its own line (or short paragraph), immediately after the greeting blank line
- Typically 1 sentence — a nicety, not substantive content
- If present, followed by a blank line before the body

### Common patterns
```
Hope you're doing well.
Hope this email finds you well.
I hope your week is going well.
How are you?
How are things?
How's everything going?
Thank you for your email.
Thank you for getting back to me.
Thanks for reaching out.
I hope you had a great weekend.
It was great seeing you at [event].
```

### Detection strategy
- Sentence immediately after greeting
- Contains: "hope you", "how are", "how's", "thanks for", "thank you for your", "great seeing you", "nice to hear from you"
- Must be short (under ~15 words typically)
- Must NOT contain substantive request/information

---

## 4. Zone 3 — Body

### Rules
- One or more paragraphs, each separated by a blank line
- First paragraph states the purpose/reason for writing
- Subsequent paragraphs provide detail, justification, context
- Final body paragraph may contain a call to action ("Please let me know", "Could you send me...")

### Paragraph detection
- Sentences that share a topic flow into the same paragraph
- Topic shifts = new paragraph (blank line)
- Questions embedded in context stay in the same paragraph

---

## 5. Zone 4 — Closing Pleasantry (Optional)

### Rules
- Separate line/short paragraph BEFORE the sign-off
- Separated from body by a blank line
- NOT the same as the sign-off — it is a pleasantry, the sign-off is a valediction

### Common patterns
```
Have a good day.
Have a great weekend.
Enjoy the rest of your week.
Looking forward to hearing from you.
Looking forward to your response.
I look forward to hearing from you.
Let me know if you have any questions.
Please don't hesitate to reach out.
Don't hesitate to reach out if you need anything.
Take care.
Talk soon.
Hope to hear from you soon.
```

### Detection keywords
```
have a good, have a great, enjoy the rest, looking forward,
look forward to, don't hesitate, do not hesitate, let me know if,
take care, talk soon, hope to hear, speak soon
```

---

## 6. Zone 5 — Sign-off / Valediction

### Rules
- Its own line
- Capitalize first word only (e.g., "Best regards," not "Best Regards,")
- Followed by a comma
- NO blank line between sign-off and name

### Common patterns (by formality)

**Formal:**
```
Sincerely,
Respectfully,
Yours truly,
Yours faithfully,
With kind regards,
```

**Semi-formal (most common in business):**
```
Best regards,
Kind regards,
Warm regards,
With best regards,
Many thanks,
With appreciation,
```

**Casual:**
```
Best,
Thanks,
Thank you,
Cheers,
Talk soon,
Take care,
All the best,
```

### Detection keywords
```
regards, sincerely, respectfully, yours truly, yours faithfully,
best, thanks, thank you, cheers, warmly, cordially, all the best
```

**Note:** "Talk soon" and "Take care" can be EITHER a closing pleasantry (Zone 4) or a sign-off (Zone 5). If followed by a name, treat as sign-off. If followed by another closing line + name, treat as pleasantry.

---

## 7. Zone 6 — Name / Signature

### Rules
- Directly below sign-off (no blank line between them)
- Name on its own line
- Optional: title on next line, company on next line
- In plain-text email, signature is just the name

### Pattern
```
Best regards,
John Smith
Marketing Director
Acme Corp
```

## 8. Concrete Templates

### Template A — Full formal email
```
Dear Mr. Thompson,
                                          ← blank line
I hope this message finds you well.
                                          ← blank line
I'm writing to follow up on our conversation about the Q3 budget proposal. The revised numbers are attached for your review.
                                          ← blank line
Please note that the deadline for approval is Friday, April 5th. If you need any additional details, I'm happy to provide them.
                                          ← blank line
Looking forward to hearing from you.
                                          ← blank line
Best regards,
Sarah Chen
Finance Manager
```

### Template B — Semi-formal (most common)
```
Hi David,
                                          ← blank line
Just wanted to check in about the project timeline. Can you send me an update by end of day?
                                          ← blank line
Thanks,
Maria
```

### Template C — Casual with pleasantries
```
Hey Lisa,
                                          ← blank line
How are things?
                                          ← blank line
I wanted to let you know the meeting has been moved to Thursday at 2pm. Same room as before.
                                          ← blank line
Have a great day.
                                          ← blank line
Best,
Jake
```

### Template D — Bare minimum
```
Hi,
                                          ← blank line
The report is ready. You can find it in the shared drive under Q3 Reports.
                                          ← blank line
Thanks,
Alex
```

### Template E — No name, casual message
```
Hi,
                                          ← blank line
Just a heads up, the server will be down for maintenance tonight from 10pm to 2am.
                                          ← blank line
Thanks
```

## 9. Structured Text — Lists

### When to use bullet lists (unordered)
- Items have NO inherent order or sequence
- Listing features, options, requirements, items
- Parallel items of equal weight

### When to use numbered lists (ordered)
- Items have a specific ORDER or SEQUENCE
- Steps in a process or procedure
- Rankings or priorities
- Items that will be referenced by number

### Detection in dictation
Speakers signal lists with:
- **Explicit markers:** "first... second... third...", "number one... number two...", "one... two... three..."
- **Bullet markers:** "bullet", "dash", "point"
- **Implicit patterns:** repeated sentence structure with short items — e.g., "we need milk, we need eggs, we need bread"
- **Introductory phrases:** "here are the items", "the following things", "there are three things", "a few points"

### List formatting rules
- Each list item on its own line
- Prefixed with `- ` (bullet) or `1. ` (numbered)
- If list follows an introductory sentence, blank line between intro and first item
- Keep list items parallel in structure (all start with verb, all noun phrases, etc.)

## 10. Section Headings

### When headings appear in dictation
- Speaker says "section", "heading", "topic", "next section", "moving on to"
- Headings are short labels (1-5 words typically)

### Formatting rules
- Heading on its own line
- Blank line before heading
- Blank line after heading (before content)
- In plain text: heading followed by colon, or ALL CAPS, or on its own line
